Carbon offset

Some greenhouse emissions seem unavoidable, but is there a way to offset them? In this article, Ollie and Shiji explain what carbon offset is, why you should consider buying it and where to buy it.

By Shiji Qin and Ollie Campbell-lendrum

|

20 Jan 2021

|

5 min

An increasingly popular method of reducing your own carbon footprint is through the purchase of carbon offsets. Carbon offsets allow people to invest money into programs that reduce greenhouse gas production or lead to an increase in carbon storage, with the aim of balancing out the emissions that they made. Someone might want to purchase carbon offsets after they have been on a trans-Atlantic flight for example. There are many different ways that this can be done.


Why should we support carbon offset?


The reason we should buy carbon offsets is obvious. During the life, there is a lot of unavoidable carbon emissions. Taking flying as an example: According to the report of ATAG in 2019, 4.5 billion passengers were carried, and that caused 915 million tons of carbon emission in 2019. To be more specific, calculated by the ICAO Carbon Emissions Calculator, for a return trip from London Heathrow Airport to JFK Airport in New York the emission of one economy class seat would be 1.8 tonnes.

Carbon offset projects can be mainly divided into two aspects: 1) Investing companies engaged in environmental protection with money by buying share of carbon offset. And the companies engaged in environmental protection would further invest money in different projects like building up photovoltaic and wind power stations in other places of the world. 2) Directly buying the carbon credits. Just like the normal products, the carbon credits are the verified offsets, which means individuals are paying for the already produced and verified oxygen. Through the above two channels, people can purchase for their unavoidable carbon emission with the sustainable energy in other parts of the world, just like paying for the CO2 they produced by, for example, flying.
