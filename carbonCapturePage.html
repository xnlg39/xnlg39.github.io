Separation of Carbon Dioxide in Power plants


There are two main types of seperation in the power plants. Pre-combustion

In pre-combustion carbon capture, CO2 is trapped before it is diluted by other flue gases. The fossil fuel is heated with pure oxygen, resulting in a mixture of hydrogen and carbon monoxide. To create carbon dioxide, which is collected, along with hydrogen, carbon monoxide is reacted with water. Therefore, it is possible to use hydrogen to create energy, and carbon dioxide is stored.


Post-combustion

The CO2 is collected immediately after the fossil fuel is burned in post-combustion carbon capture. In essence, CO2 is isolated by bubbling the gas into an absorber column filled with liquid solvents, such as ammonia, from the flue gas that contains CO2, water vapor, sulfur dioxides and nitrogen oxides. In the most commonly used method, a stream of superheated steam at about 120 degrees is passed through it until the chemicals in the absorber column become saturated. This frees up the trapped CO2, which can then be transported elsewhere for storage.


Main carbon capture methods


Carbon capture and storage (CCS) includes the processes of capturing waste carbon dioxide (CO2) from the air, transporting the captured CO2 to a storage site and then depositing it where it can not enter the atmosphere. The two most prevalent techniques for carbon capture are Direct Air Capture and Bio-Energy with Carbon Capture and Storage, both of which will be introduced below. most of the captured CO2, is injected into sedimentary basins, requiring an impermeable cap rock to prevent the CO2 from migrating to the surface, for example, a depleted oil or gas field or a deep rock reservoir beneath the sea. Alternatively, captured carbon can also be deposited into reactive rocks (such as mafic or ultramafic lithologies) by injection, provoking CO2 mineralization and, thereby, permanently fixing carbon with negligible risk of return to the atmosphere.


Direct air capture

As the name suggests, Direct Air Capture (DAC) is a technology to capture the CO2 directly from the air. Just like trees breathing, DAC technology would draw air into the machine, after which the pull-in atmosphere would past through a device called an ‘Air Contactor’ where the carbon capture actually happens. Then the most of CO2 in the captured air would be turned into solid Calcium Carbonate or directly into liquid through a series of chemical reactions while returning the rest of the air back to the world. The captured CO2 would then be buried or transported to be used in other industries. Taking the Carbon Engineering Company, which is backed by Bill Gates, as an example, only one plant of the company with DAC technology does the work of 40 million trees in the world.

This structure can wet scrub the air by using a strong hydroxide solution to capture CO2 and convert it into carbonate. The hydroxide solution reacts with CO2 to form carbonate ions. This occurs within a structure which is much the same as an industrial cooling tower.

 
K2CO3 + CaOH → CaCO3 + 2KOH
 

The carbonate ion reacts with calcium (Ca2+) to form calcium carbonate, in the form of dried pellets.

 
CaCO3 + Heat → CaO + CO2
 

A circulating fluid heats the calcium carbonate pellets to decomposition temperature, breaking them apart to release the carbon dioxide as a gas and leave behind calcium oxide (CaO).

 
8CO + 17H2 → C8H18 + 8H2O
 

This is a process where a mixture of carbon monoxide and hydrogen are converted into liquid hydrocarbons. These reactions occur in the presence of metal catalysts and typically at temperatures of 150℃-300℃. The carbon dioxide is combined with hydrogen and converted into liquid fuels, including gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel, using the Fischer-Tropsch process.



Bio-Energy with Carbon Capture and Storage

Bio-Energy with Carbon Capture and Storage (BECCS) is a way to combat climate change with photosynthesis but it is more complicated than just planting trees. BECCS is the process of extracting bioenergy from biomass and capturing and storing the carbon to remove it from the atmosphere. For the most common example, the energy plants, like trees with a short growth cycle and crops, will be planted and grow. And, as the plants grow, they will absorb the CO2 out of the air. Then, once the plants are grown, carbon will be contained in the plants while biomass is extracted for biofuel production. After that, the biofuel can be used to power cars, planes or even industries, and the carbon is capture to be stored or can be used to make products like concrete.



Uses of captured carbon


Most of captured CO2 would be made into Calcium Carbonate through few chemical processes as for it would easier to be transported because actually there is a number of industries need CO2 as a raw material. For the most common examples: CO2 is used in beverage industry to make products like coke; in fire extinguishers it is used as a refrigerant; also promoting the growth of plants in greenhouses. However, with the innovation of the technology, CO2 can be used as a raw material to make oil.

According to an article published on Nature Communications on May 2nd 2017, a catalyst for making high-octane gasoline directly from hydrogen and carbon dioxide was invented, which means the most important greenhouse gas, CO2, can be used as an source of energy.
