Personal impact on the climate

10 min

When looking at global warming it can be easy to think that our actions are futile in the uphill battle against climate change. The reality is that we as individuals have all the power. The global warming we see today is a result of decisions made by individuals. In 2020 the average carbon footprint of a person in the UK was about 12 tonnes of CO2e. In this article, I will explain how an individual can potentially drop their carbon footprint to 5.2 tonnes with little extra hassle. I will explore the four main areas that impact the environment and how to either remove them completely or reduce them significantly.

The first pie shows the average carbon emission of an individual in the UK in 2020 (12 tonnes) and The second shows the estimated reduction following actions layed out in this article (5.22 tonnes) on the right. Click here to see how these were calculated.


Energy


The average CO2 emission per person in the UK due to electricity is roughly 2.5 tonnes. This works out to be about 20% of an average individuals carbon footprint. This is a significant chunk. However, it is easy to quantify, reduce and save yourself money in the process!

The first and most significant change you can make is to switch to a green energy provider. In the Uk, we already have multiple options, many of which are cheaper than their biggest competitors. Take pure planet as an example. They charge a monthly membership fee of around £10/month and then sell you electricity at the price they buy it from the grid. For individuals who consume a lot of energy, it works out significantly cheaper as a result. Here is a link to the best green energy providers in the UK as of 2021.

Now that all your electricity is green, your carbon footprint due to electricity is now zero, reducing your usage simply reduces your bill. however, for the sake of completeness, it is worth mentioning some ways to reduce electricity consumption. The obvious one we have all heard about is LED's, in fact switching your house over to LED's will pay itself off in less than a year on the lower end of usage (4hrs/day) and they will last far longer. Modern electrical appliances are more efficient than older ones as a rule however this must be taken with a pinch of salt. Although replacing your household with brand new electrical appliances would be fun, the effect this shopping spree will have on the environment will be significant and likely outweigh any increase in efficiency. Finally, switching to smart device management allows for more control of appliances. Things like lights, heating, television and more can be switched on and off at set times and from your phone. This can make sure appliances are not left on and wasting precious energy.


Heating


Heating also makes up a large proportion of an individual's carbon footprint. The average carbon footprint of an individual in the UK due to heating is roughly 1.8 tonnes. This works out to be about 15% of an average individuals carbon footprint. Reducing the emissions here can be split into two main sections. Heat production and heat retention/management of the home. Both are important when it comes to reducing the greenhouse gas production however it is rather expensive to change your method of heat production and although it would likely pay itself off in the long run, it can be a large investment up front.


Heat production

Currently, there are lots of exciting ways to heat your house and water. Although it varies from house to house a Heat pump is probably the best way to heat your home. In conjunction with a green energy plan, your heatings carbon footprint would become zero due to the fact that a heat pump runs off of electricity. They are expensive to install and the version that is right for you is largely dependent on the house it is being installed in. This is a far better option than electrical radiators and an electric boiler. Although this would also be 100% green it would also cost far more to run. This form of electric heating is very inefficient and your monthly bill will reflect this.


A more in-depth breakdown of the options including all of the heat pumps available can be found in this article by Friends of the earth.

Heat retention and management

Modern-day insulation has come on leaps and bounds over the last decade, providing lots of fantastic options for your home. Once again price must be considered, one can upgrade their house entirely with perfect insulation and this will make a massive improvement to their heat retention, however, this would also come at a large price. It is likely worth hiring an energy performance assessor to look at your house, it will cost around £50 and from there, you will be able to see the most efficient places to spend your money. A list of accredited assessors can be found here.

Once you know which part of your house is loosing the most heat, you can focus your efforts there. A simple google search of cheap insulation for any part of your home will provide all you need. Options such as insulated wallpaper won't break the bank but they can make a noticeable difference. Improving your homes heat retention in this way will not only be better for the environment, but over the long term, it will save you alot of money.

One of the best purchases you can make for your home's heating is smart controls. They allow for easy management of your heating and electrical system. Most can be controlled from your phone or laptop and can make adjustments to your heating schedule based on information such as weather and if you are at home or not. This study into smart controls showed an average reduction of 5% on heating bills. This means as an investment they will pay themselves off, reduce your heating bill and in-turn your carbon footprint.


Transport


Transport, can be a difficult area of global warming to quantify on an individual basis. Peoples travel habits vary drastically and the benefits of things such as public transport are difficult to measure with great accuracy.


Cars

When looking at cars there is no perfect option glaring one in the face. Clearly moving away from old more inefficient gas guzzlers will help reduce your carbon footprint however the best alternative is unclear. Running an electric car on green energy is the best option on paper currently, however, estimates suggest that there are not enough raw resources on earth to produce enough of the parts to provide the world with electric cars. Furthermore, from an ethical standpoint, the mining conditions in the Congo may allow for cheap production of Cobalt (a key ingredient in batteries) but at a sizeable ethical cost! Hydrogen fuel would be the panacea the world needs; however, in its current state, it is too expensive, and the infrastructure is not in place to support the worlds current demand for travel.


Moving away from single-passenger cars to other means of travel is definitely a good thing. Although hard to quantify how much better for the environment a bus is over a car it is clear that they are better. Cycling and walking are not only better for the environment but better for you as well. Carpooling can also be a fantastic way to reduce your footprint.

Walking and cycling when at all possible is a great option. Carpooling and public transport can help reduce your emissions even further. If you need a car and you can afford a good electric/hybrid car then that in conjunction with green electricity is one of the best options available for you right now.


Aviation

Aviation makes up roughly 2.5% of the global carbon emission in a year however only a small percentage of the world population actually fly. This means for those who do fly it will make up a large proportion of your carbon footprint.

A return flight from London to San Fransisco emits around 2.8 tonnes of Co2 equivalent per seat on that flight. To put this value in perspective, this is 23% of an average persons yearly carbon footprint. This means cutting down on flights, especially long-distance ones would go along way in reducing your impact on the environment. We simply don't need to travel around the world to enjoy ourselves. I recently went on a caravan tour of the UK and I must say we have everything we need right on our doorstep.


Most people will still fly at some point in their life. There are still ways to offset this very impactful activity. Carbon offset certificates can be purchased from companies that work to counteract carbon emission. Follow this link to view our page on carbon offset.


Food


The topic of food and climate change is a heavily disputed subject and from my own experience, most articles online are people pushing their own agenda onto others. I will try to avoid this and keep it clear and concise. Consumption of any kind has an effect on the environment, whether you eat the greenest diet or not the food you eat comes at a carbon cost. This carbon emission on a given item of food is often hard to quantify due to the complicated nature of the question. With little quantification, however, we can still make some general statements of what is better for the environment and what isn't.


Meat

Probably the single most debated topic when looking at foods effect on the climate. All animals create carbon dioxide as they respire and some Animals such as Ruminants produce methane during their digestion process which is a very potent greenhouse gas (learn more in the drop-down bellow). Furthermore, Animals must eat food in order to create edible protein. Vegans and vegetarians make the argument that this is inefficient and Humans could simply eat the plant matter themself skipping the middle man and removing the methane and carbon dioxide that the animals create as a by-product. This, on paper, seems a valid argument however it is not the full picture. Arguments can be made for humans evolving to have meat in their diet and hence to be healthy we require certain essential nutrients that can only be provided from meat. Furthermore, there is a growing body of research looking at regenerative farming. This involves farming in a more natural way i.e. animals and crops forming beneficial relationships. This research suggests that soil regeneration and as a result, carbon storage can be achieved with a more responsible approach to farming. This paper suggests that regenerative farming can offset the entire travel sectors carbon footprint.

Regardless of which side of the fence you stand, ruminant meat is impactful on the environment. This paper looks at the carbon emission of lots of different foods. It compared the carbon emission of meats and vegetables per 100g of protein. Beef and Sheep produce significantly more greenhouse gas than other meats. Per 100g of beef protein, the mean CO2 equivalent was 50kg. This works out to roughly 14kg of CO2e per portion of beef. The same portion of chicken would only emit 1.6kg of CO2e. It must be noted that this paper has looked at current farming practices and looks at what is currently available in shops. These figures would likely be different when considering local meat, farmed using regenerative practices.

It is still clear that a general reduction in meat consumption (especially Ruminants meat) is good for the environment. In 2020 the average person in the UK consumed roughly 62KG of meat. this works out to about 1.2KG of meat per week which is 2 servings of meat a day. Meat is very good for you when eaten in moderation but the keyword here is moderation. Reducing meat consumption by half of the national average would take you to 1 meat serving a day and although hard to quantify exactly it will definitely reduce the strain on the environment. Also by eating less meat, individuals can afford to buy more ethically sourced meat. Although this is outside of the scope of this site, it is not hard to make an argument for why this is a good thing!

Ruminants are herbivorous animals that are able to acquire nutrients from plant-based foods by fermenting them in a designated stomach prior to digestion. One of the main problems with Ruminants is a by-product of this fermentation process: Methane. Methane is thought to be about 80x more warming than CO2 however one should keep in mind that Methane in the upper-atmosphere undergoes free-radical reactions which break it down into CO2 and water over the space of a decade. Hence, although initially very potent after a decade its damage is drastically mitigated.

The atmospheric breakdown of methane makes up 90% of the total global Methane sink mechanism according to this paper. As the Methane reaches the upper-atmosphere it is subject to free-radicals. Free radicals are atoms, molecules, or ions that have unpaired valence electrons. This unpaired valence electron makes them far more reactive. These free-radicals are created by molecules and atoms being bombarded by sunlight in the upper atmosphere. These higher energy photons from the sun (that have not yet been filtered out by the atmosphere) can energise the molecules and atoms to form free radicals. The main atmospheric sink of methane is oxidation by hydroxyl radicals (HO) which has multiple mechanisms but all end with less potent greenhouse gases. The net reaction seen in these equations is actually a sum of multiple complex reactions, however, these are a little complex for this site. Those who are interested in the whole process can find out more here.

 
CH4 + 4O2 → HCHO + 2O3 + H2O
 
 
CH4 + O2 → HCHO + H2O
 

Other reactions breakdown methane in the stratosphere such as reactions with Chlorine and excited atmospheric oxygen atoms forming more complex by-products.

It must be noted that Ruminants are not the only species that create methane. In fact, wetlands host the perfect environment for microbes that produce methane. Rice paddies often replicate these conditions and themselves generate a large quantity of methane. See the picture below which shows the sources and sinks of methane.


Local Produce

Sourcing one's food more locally is not only good for the environment, it is also good for your local community. Buying local produce has a drastically reduced footprint due to the minimised mileage on the goods. Although here in the UK our crops are quite seasonal, there is still plenty of produce year-round. Follow this link to see a seasonal guide to fruit and veg in the UK. I personally get great pleasure knowing the animals and crops I see in fields near me are those on my plate.


Conclusion


Hopefully, having got this far you are filled with confidence that you have the power to save the environment. To recap on how to reduce your carbon footprint by roughly 60%:

  • Energy: 2.5 → 0 tonnes

    Moving to a green energy provider will completly remove your energy footprint but most likely save you money in the process.

  • Heating: 1.5 → 0 tonnes

    This is based off of a heat pump in conjunction with a green energy plan. However more efficient retention of heat would make a drastic impact for those not looking to purchase a new boiler.

  • Transport: 3.2 → 1.6 tonnes

    This can be acheived by looking to utilise carpooling, public transport, cycling and walking. It must be noted that moving to an electric car with a green enery plan would result in a reduction to zero.

  • Food: 1.2 → 0.6 tonnes

    Looking to buy more local produce and reduce meat consumption by half.

The above examples should be taken with a pinch of salt. Some will be able to implement all of the above and potentially go further. Others will not be able to afford a new heat pump and stop using their car. This article should be more of a guide on how one can, and should, take their personal decisions seriously. The effect of everyone in the UK simply halving their footprint would put the UK 20 years ahead of schedule on the UK's climate roadmap outlined in the Paris agreement. What stands between that, and where we are now, is action!
