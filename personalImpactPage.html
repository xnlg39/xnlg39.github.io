Energy


The average CO2 emission per person in the UK due to electricity is roughly 1.5 tonnes. This works out to be about 10% of an average individuals carbon footprint. This is a significant chunk. However, it is easy to quantify, reduce and save yourself money in the process!

The first and most significant change you can make is to switch to a green energy provider. In the Uk, we already have multiple options, many of which are cheaper than their biggest competitors. Take pure planet as an example. They charge a monthly membership fee of around £10/month and then sell you electricity at the price they buy it from the grid. For individuals who consume a lot of energy, it works out significantly cheaper as a result. Here is a link to the best green energy providers in the UK as of 2021.

Now that all your electricity is green, your carbon footprint due to electricity is now zero, reducing your usage simply reduces your bill. however, for the sake of completeness, it is worth mentioning some ways to reduce electricity consumption. The obvious one we have all heard about is LED's, in fact switching your house over to LED's will pay itself off in less than a year on the lower end of usage (4hrs/day) and they will last far longer. Modern electrical appliances are more efficient than older ones as a rule however this must be taken with a pinch of salt. Although replacing your household with brand new electrical appliances would be fun, the effect this shopping spree will have on the environment will be significant and likely outweigh any increase in efficiency. Finally, switching to smart device management allows for more control of appliances. Things like lights, heating, television and more can be switched on and off at set times and from your phone. This can make sure appliances are not left on and wasting precious energy.


Heating


Heating also makes up a large proportion of an individual's carbon footprint. The average carbon footprint of an individual in the UK due to heating is roughly 1.5 tonnes. Reducing the emissions here can be split into two main sections. Heat production and heat retention/management of the home. Both are important when it comes to reducing the greenhouse gas production however it is rather expensive to change your method of heat production and although it would likely pay itself off in the long run, it can be a large investment up front.

Heat production

Currently, there are lots of exciting ways to heat your house and water. Although it varies from house to house a Heat pump is probably the best way to heat your house. In conjunction with a green energy plan, your heatings carbon footprint would become zero due to the fact that a heat pump runs off of electricity. They are expensive to install and the version that is right for you is largely dependent on the house it is being installed in.

If you are on a green energy plan it would be tempting to heat the house and water with electrical radiators and an electric boiler. Although this would be 100% green it would also cost far more than the other options. Electric heating is very inefficient and your monthly bill will reflect this.

A more in-depth breakdown of the options including all of the heat pumps available can be found in this article by Friends of the earth.

Heat retention and management

Modern-day insulation has come on leaps and bounds over the last decade, providing lots of fantastic options for your home. Once again price must be considered, one can upgrade their house entirely with perfect insulation and will make a massive improvement to their heat retention, however, this would come at a large price. It is likely worth hiring an energy performance assessor to look at your house, it will cost around £50 and from there, you will be able to see the most efficient places to spend your money. A list of accredited assessors can be found here. Once you know which part of your house is loosing the most precious heat you can focus your energy there. A simple google search of cheap insulation for blank will provide all you need. Options such as insulated wallpaper won't break the bank but they will make a difference on brick walls.

One of the best purchases you can make for your home's heating is smart controls. They allow for easy management of your heating system. Most can be controlled from your phone or laptop and can make adjustments to your heating schedule based on information such as weather and if you are at home. This study into smart controls showed a 5% reduction in heating bills on average which means as an investment they will pay themselves off and reduce your heating bill and in-turn your carbon footprint.

Food


The topic of food and climate change is a heavily disputed subject and it is mostly people pushing their own agenda onto others. I will try to avoid this and keep it clear and concise. Consumption of any kind has an effect on the environment, whether you eat the greenest diet or not the food you eat comes at a carbon cost. This carbon emission on a given item of food is often hard to pinpoint due to the complicated nature of the question. Without looking into this, however, we can make some general statements of what is better for the environment and what isn't.


Meat

Probably the single most debated topic when looking at foods effect on the climate. All animals create carbon dioxide as they respire and other Animals such as Ruminants produce methane during their digestion process which is a very potent greenhouse gas. Furthermore, Animals must eat food in order to create edible protein. Vegans and vegetarians make the argument that this is inefficient and Humans could simply eat the plant matter themself skipping the middle man and removing the methane and carbon dioxide that the animals create as a by-product. This on paper seems a valid argument however it is not the full picture. Arguments can be made for humans evolving to have meat in their diet and hence to be healthy we require certain essential nutrients that can only be provided from meat. Furthermore, there is a growing mound of research looking at regenerative farming. This involves farming in a more natural way i.e. Animals and crops forming beneficial relationships. This research suggests that soil regeneration and as a result, carbon storage can be achieved with a more responsible approach to farming.

Regardless of which side of the fence you stand, it is still clear that a general reduction in meat consumption is good for the environment. In 2020 the average person in the UK consumed roughly 62KG of meat. this works out to about 1.2KG of meat per week which is 2 servings of meat a day. Although an exact value is hard to come up with it comes down to comfort. Meat is very good for you when eaten in moderation but the keyword here is moderation. Reducing meat consumption by half of the national average would take you to 1 meat serving a day and although hard to quantify it will reduce the strain on the environment. Also by eating less meat, individuals can afford to buy more ethically sourced meat. Although this is outside of the scope of this site, it is not hard to make an argument for why this is a good thing.

Ruminants are herbivorous animals that are able to acquire nutrients from plant-based foods by fermenting them in a designated stomach prior to digestion. One of the main problems with Ruminants is a by-product of this fermentation process: Methane. Methane is thought to be about 80x more warming than CO2 however one should keep in mind that Methane in the upper-atmosphere undergoes free-radical reactions which break it down into CO2 and water over the space of a decade[]. Hence, although initially very potent after a decade its damage is drastically mitigated.

The atmospheric breakdown of methane makes up 90% of the total global Methane sink mechansim[]. As the Methane reaches the upper-atmosphere it is subject to free-radicals. Free radicals are atoms, molecules, or ions that have unpaired valence electrons. This unpaired valence electron makes them far more reactive. These free-radicals are created by molecules and atoms being bombarded by sunlight in the upper atmosphere. These higher energy photons from the sun (that have not been filtered out by the atmosphere) can energise the molecules and atoms to form free radicals. The main atmospheric sink of methane is oxidation by hydroxyl radicals (HO) which has multiple mechanisms but all end with less potent greenhouse gases.
