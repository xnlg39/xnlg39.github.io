Climate activism

What can we do to change the minds of those who make big decisions? In this page, Ollie explores the possible options we have as individuals to change the minds of our leaders. Everything from voting to protests to Greta Thunberg and more...

By Ollie Campbell-lendrum

|

9th Feb 2021

|

6 min


There are many lifestyle changes that a person can make to reduce their own personal impact on the climate, however, global warming is not the fault of one single individual, and nor can it be resolved through the actions of one single person. Large amounts of greenhouse gas emissions are caused by the decisions of corporations and governments. Since 1988 one hundred of the world's largest companies have been responsible for 71% of the greenhouse gas emissions [1]. Despite this, there are still ways that an individual can influence corporations and governments to change the way in which they operate.


Public Consensus


Public Consensus on climate change in September 2020

Recent opinion polls demonstrate that the public is becoming increasingly concerned about climate change and its effects. As of September 2020, 82% of people in Britain described themselves as either very concerned (38%) or fairly concerned (44%) about climate change, this was a 4% rise from March of that year [2]. 71% of the world's population believe that the threat posed by climate change is as serious a crisis as the covid-19 pandemic [3]. Concern about climate change is highest amongst young people with 46% of British 18-24-year-olds describing themselves as very concerned, this is in contrast with only 24% of people over the age of 65 [4].


What can an individual do?


In a democratic country if an individual is not happy about the way their government is handling climate change they can vote for a candidate or party that they believe will do a better job at tackling the issue. Voting for a political party with climate change-focused policies also sends a signal to other parties that this is an issue that is important to voters and may result in them changing their position for future elections. However for this to work and for there to be a change in a country's environmental policies requires a large number of people to share the same beliefs and have the same desire for change.

Many of those who are most worried about climate change and its impact are younger people who do not yet have the right to vote and therefore can not directly have an impact on the governance of a country, yet there are still ways that they can have an impact on the democratic process. Writing to a local representative, such as an MP or congressperson, is an effective way of demonstrating to politicians that climate change is an issue that the younger generation of potential future voters is concerned about, and will hold them accountable for in future elections. Whilst not having the right to vote themselves young people do have influence over people who do, such as their parents, they can therefore persuade their parents to vote in a way that reflects their beliefs.

Protesting is another method that an individual can use to exercise their democratic rights and demonstrate their displeasure towards how climate change is being tackled. Protesting comes in many forms whether that be the more peaceful Fridays for Future school strikes started by Swedish teenager Greta Thunberg or the "civil disobedience" of Extinction Rebellion [5][6].

Boycotting products or even whole industries is an effective method of punishing companies that are not doing enough to limit their own carbon footprint. For example, from 2016-2019, 35 of the world's largest banks invested $2.7 trillion into fossil fuel companies, with JPMorgan being the biggest investor in the industry worldwide and Barclays in Europe [7]. In addition to limiting their own use of fossil fuels, individuals can stop keeping their money with banks that continue to fund the world's biggest polluters.


Case studies


Greta Thunberg - Impact of an Individual

Perhaps the most well known example of how an individual can have an impact on climate policy is that of the Swedish teenage activist Greta Thunberg.


In August 2018, when she was 15, Greta Thunberg began striking from school and protesting outside the Swedish parliament building, every day for three weeks, demanding that the Swedish government honour the pledges on reducing greenhouse emissions made in the Paris Agreement (link) From October of that year, she gave a number of high profile public speeches, including a TEDx talk in Stockholm (link) As international media coverage on her grew she inspired a number of similar protests around the world, including school strikes attended by thousands of people in Australia during November and the first Fridays for Future march in Germany during December [Greta 3][Greta 4]. Just a few months after she began her protests, in December, she gave a speech to the COP24 United Nations Climate Change summit, in which she addressed world leaders and told them that they were "not mature enough to tell it like it is" [Greta 5]. On Friday of March 2019 over a million schoolchildren took part in climate protests in 125 different countries [Greta 6]. Throughout 2019, Greta Thunberg attended various protests around Europe and gave public speeches to governments and world leaders, and in August she sailed across the Atlantic on a carbon-neutral yacht in order to attend UN climate conferences in the Americas [Greta 7]. In 2019 she was named as the youngest ever TIME person of the year [Greta 8].

Whilst it is difficult to measure the direct impact of Greta Thunberg's words and actions, there are some signs that she has made a difference to environmental policy across many countries, mostly in Europe. When speaking to Thunberg, in 2019, then British Environment minister said that he felt "responsibility and guilt" due to her words, German chancellor Angela Merkel also said that Greta Thunberg drove her government to act on climate change [Greta 9][Greta 10]. The then president of the European Commison, Jean-Claude Juncker, also announced alongside Thunberg, in 2019, that "from 2021 to 2027 every fourth euro spent within the EU budget will go towards action to mitigate climate change", this amounts to one trillion euros over seven years [Greta 11]. During the European Parliament elections in May 2019, seats occupied by Green parties increased from 52 to 72, with most of this increase occuring in northern Europe, where young climate protestors were most active [Greta 12]. Away from politics, a study published in 2021 demonstrates that Americans "who are more familiar with Greta Thunberg have higher intentions of taking collective actions to reduce global warming" [Greta 13].

Extinction Rebellion - Collective Impact

Extinction Rebellion is an international environmental protest group, established mostly by British academics, which protests for change in climate policy through "nonviolent civil disobedience" [XR 1].

The group was founded in October 2018 and launched a "declaration of rebellion" on the 31st when more than 1000 protestors blocked roads around the Houses of Parliament and assembled in Parliament Square [XR 2]. On what the group called "rebellion day", on the 17th November, 6000 activists blocked access to the five main bridges across the river Thames before again marching on Parliament Square [XR 3]. Throughout the remainder of 2018 and the first few months of 2019, there were further disruptions across the country including an occupation of the Scottish Parliament building and attempts to disrupt London Fashion Week [XR 4][XR 5]. The protests culminated in a series of occupations around London starting from the 15th of April, including blocking Waterloo Bridge for a week, activists gluing themselves to buildings, and placing a pink boat in the intersection of Oxford and Regent Street, leading to thousands of arrests [XR 6].

The impact of the protests can be seen soon after the events of the 15th of April. At the end of the month London Mayor met with senior figures in the movement and pledged to consider a citizens assembly on climate change, which was one of the groups key demands [XR 7]. On the 1st of May the British Parliament declared a climate emergency, becoming the first country in the world to do so [XR 8]. MPs then agreed, in June, to establish a citizens aseembly, made up of a sample of the population, to discuss the climate crisis [XR 9].
